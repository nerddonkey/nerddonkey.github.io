 Planet Astrophotography

Goal

Explore the wonders of the Universe and leave Earth for the narrow minded people. Target the Solar System (mostly Jupiter, Saturn and Mars) and elsewhere look at Deep Space objects.

Equipment

  • You need big aperture (20cm +) and long focal length so a Cassegrain/SCT and a Barlow/Powermate work best.
  • The physical length of camera and opticals as short as possible
  • Spacing for the Powermate should be as close as possible to recommended distance to sensor (56mm)
  • You don’t need good off-axis optical performance – a standard SCT will perform as well as an EdgeHD or ACF (low off-axis coma scopes).

Operational/Observational Aspects

  • Collimate your telescope; then collimate again because it has to be perfect.
  • With a SCT, lock down your primary focus (which moves the main mirror); if you change this setting then you’ll probably need to repeat collimation.
  • At the high magnification required the atmospheric dispersion can be significant so shoot the planet away from the horizon; ideally around the meridian. Atmospheric dispersion correctors do work but are fiddly to adjust and need continupus adjustment as the planet changes altitude.

Components (in order from telescope towards camera)

  1. GSO Crayford Focuser for SCT 10:1 (2 Inch) – M-SCT001
    • to enable motorized focusing (with a Sento Senso)
    • 1.25” adapter is included and used with the above focuser
  2. Sento Senso – SESTOSENSO
    • robotic focusing motor
    • can compensate for focal shift with different filters (even if they are meant to be parfocal – they are never exactly parfocal)
  3. Tele Vue Powermate 2.5x – PMT-2513
    • one of the most highly regarded image magnifiers
  4. Tele Vue 1.25” Powermate T-Ring Adapter – PTR-1250
    • Camera-side T-ring shoulder is what we take as the optical reference point 0mm
    • Camera-side thread: T2 male (M42x0.75)
    • Optical reference: 0mm
  5. TS Optics T2 quick changer and 360° rotator – TST2Rot
    • Enables one point of rotational adjustment of camera axes (with telescope axes)
    • Telescope-side thread: T2 female (M42x0.75)
    • Camera-side thread: T2 male (M42x0.75)
    • Optical length (w/o T2 male thread): 5.5mm
    • Additional Optical length due to Powermate T-Ring Adapter standoff: 2.25mm
  6. ZWO 21mm T2 to T2 Extension T-Ring Spacer – SKU: ZWO-EFW-8x1.25
    • Spacer to get near the correct backfocus for the Powermate (T-Ring Adapter shoulder to the sensor)
    • Telescope-side thread: T2 female (M42x0.75)
    • Camera-side thread: T2 male (M42x0.75)
    • Optical length (w/o T2 male thread): 21mm
  7. ZWO EFW Filter Wheel 8×1.25” – SKU: ZWO-EFW-8x1.25
    • Uses the new ZWO LRGB filter set
    • Telescope-side thread: T2 female (M42x0.75)
    • Camera-side thread: T2 female (M42x0.75)
    • Optical length: 20mm
  8. ZWO Anti-dew heater strip (not used; for DSO setup) – SKU: ZWO-Anti-dew
    • Optical length: ~0.5mm
  9. ZWO ASI183MM Pro (cooling not used; for DSO setup) – SKU: ASI183MM-P
    • Resolution: 5496 pixels x 3672 pixels (20.18 MP)
    • Sensor Size: 13.19mm x 8.8mm
    • Pixel Size: 2.4μm x 2.4μm
    • Telescope-side thread: T2 male (M42x0.75)
    • Optical length (w/o T2 male thread and to sensor): 6.5mm (back focus)

Spacings

Powermate–Sensor Separation

With all these bits and bobs the distance from the Powermate T-Ring Adapter to the sensor is:

5.5mm + 2.25mm + 21mm + 20mm + 0.5mm + 6.5mm = 55.75mm

This is very close to the recommended value of 56mm. But it is clear from Tele Vue magnification information that the powermates are quite tolerant to a variation in spacings.

(Based on actual measurements when imaging Saturn the powermate is running at a magnification of 2.4x. Comparing to Tele Vue magnification information the top surface of the powermate eyepiece holder is at an effective distance of 41mm relative to where the Powermate T-Ring Adapter camera-side shoulder would be so we seem to be operating at 15mm on the plot and that appears to be around 2.4x.)

Rear-cell–Powermate Separation

It isn’t clear from any documentation what is Meade’s preferred/design position of the focal plane relative to the SCT rear cell (actually the camera-side shoulder of the 2"-24 adapter), which is adjusted by the primary mirror focuser. One rough guide is the Meade camera T-adapter which has an optical length of 45mm which when used with an EOS T-ring and EOS Canon SLR camera body (backfocus of 44mm) should give:

45mm + 11mm + 44mm = 100mm

Now because the Crayford focuser is not so shallow our effective focal plane mights be at around 150mm, which is something that can be checked at the telescope (by projecting the moon to a translucent film when the powermate is removed). The powermate–Sensor separation should be more important than the rear-cell–powermate separation.
